My Security Project: GrapheneOS

One of the first things I did after studying the basics was to get some hands-on experience with a privacy and security-centric operating system: GrapheneOS. After setting it up myself, I decided to offer de-googling services for devices to others in my community, advertising through a local online marketplace. Below is a walkthrough that I follow, along with some settings that I prefer based on my experience and research, to make it both easier and more secure to use.

Installing GrapheneOS

First, you need to install GrapheneOS. You can follow the official Web installer or go directly to the Install page.

Why Use GrapheneOS?

There are a handful of reasons people use GrapheneOS, which I categorize as follows:

  • Protecting your privacy and data from third-party apps.
  • De-googling your phone due to Google’s poor track record in handling personal data.
  • Security-sensitive professions, such as ones that deal with critical infrastructure or journalism.
  • Belief in the importance of keeping your personal data private.

User Profiles

After installing GrapheneOS, I like to create multiple Android user profiles to maximize privacy and security. Although this is not exclusive to GrapheneOS, it allows me to isolate different aspects of my digital life. Here are the user profiles I typically create:

  • Primary: Admin account for managing security settings and other profiles.
  • Me: For work or volunteering. Avoid using real names for profiles.
  • Private: For web surfing, learning, and socializing.
  • Google Services: Contain Google-related apps to a single profile.
  • Finance: Keep investments, banking, and billing info separate from everything else.
  • Guest: For allowing others to use your device without compromising security.

To enable multiple users, go to Settings > System > Multiple Users > Allow multiple users.

Deleting User Profiles

Deleting profiles is useful for privacy, especially when traveling to countries with authoritarian governments. If you're in a sensitive field, such as journalism or activism, removing specific profiles beforehand could protect your privacy.

Network Settings

Navigate to Settings > Network & Internet > Internet > Network Preferences and disable "Turn on Wi-Fi automatically" and "Notify for public networks" to avoid rogue access points. For additional security, change your hotspot name and ensure no personal information is included. Choose a strong but accessible password.

Connected Devices/Bluetooth

In Connected Devices, change your device name to something non-identifiable. While GrapheneOS disables Wi-Fi automatically, you should also manually turn off Bluetooth and NFC when not in use.

Notifications & Battery

I turn off sensitive notifications so they don’t appear on the lock screen. For easier battery management, I like to enable battery percentage display.

Storage Scopes

One of the best GrapheneOS features is Storage Scopes, which allows each application to have its own folder. This feature sandbox apps, preventing them from accessing unnecessary data.

Privacy and Security

Turn off sensors permissions for apps as it is on by default, also camera and microphone access, and show clipboard access for better privacy. Disabling "Show password" adds another layer of security. All of is is better turned off.

PIN or Biometrics?

There’s debate about which is better, but I find the risk of people stealing my password through shoulder surfing greater than the risk of being forced to use biometrics by crinimals or law enforcement. So I prefer biometrics.

Changing Quick Settings

For convenience, I like to have camera and microphone access toggles in Quick Settings located directly on top of the menu. This provides peace of mind during sensitive events, such as work meetings or legal discussions.

Installing Applications

While F-Droid is popular, I prefer the NEO Store for its security features and cleaner interface. For downloading Google Play apps anonymously, I use the Aurora Store, though it sometimes has issues with the download anonymously feature. It may just require some playing with to achieve results.

Google Play Services

For certain apps, you’ll need to sandbox Google Play Services. To do this, go to Apps > Install Google Play Services on your Google user profile.

Other Thoughts

I was taught to use browsers like Vanadium without app installation permissions once initial setup is complete. To disable Vanadium’s app installation capabilities, go to Apps > Vanadium > Install Unknown Apps and toggle it off. I use Privacy Browser to download apps instead.

In closing, I'm glad I chose this as my first personal project. It has taught me how to use devices more securely and has allowed me to help others improve their digital privacy. It gives me the ability to constantly work on devices, and meet others who take their privacy seriously. For me as someone new to the field, that is really exciting.
